</div></div>					<p>In case you haven&apos;t been keeping up with the news, the owners of beloved Long Island City street art mecca <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509205235/http://gothamist.com/tags/5pointz">5Pointz</a> are planning on demolishing the warehouse and turning it into a pair of deluxe residential towers by the end of the year. Oh sure, they&apos;re promising that there will be a few &quot;art walls&quot; set aside for graffiti artists to squiggle upon, but it won&apos;t be the same, and the artists know that. But did you know that there is a different <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509205235/http://www.bushwickfivepoints.com/">Five Points</a> in Bushwick? <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509205235/http://gothamist.com/2012/10/23/hanksy_pays_homage_to_the_huxtable.php">We saw Hanksy&apos;s pieces there last year</a>.</p>

<p>It lies near the border between Brooklyn and Queens, about a block away from Ridgewood. It&apos;s not directly related to 5Pointz&#x2014;it&apos;s name stems from the five streets that converge there&#x2014;but like it&apos;s more famous namesake, it&apos;s also becoming a haven for graffiti artists. </p>

<p>Five Points is curated by Joseph Ficalora, who <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509205235/http://streetartnyc.org/blog/2012/10/08/speaking-to-joseph-ficalora-curator-of-bushwick-five-points/">explained to StreetArtNYC</a> how he gets artists access the walls along Scott Avenue, Troutman Street, and St. Nicholas Avenue: &quot;Some are family-owned and others I&#x2019;ve negotiated with their owners. Once these building owners see a finished wall, it is not difficult for them to recognize that the artwork surfacing here is far more appealing than random tags and unspeakable words!&quot;</p>

<p>Check out some fantastic photos of the artwork there.</p>
