					<p>Susan Gardner started <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170203043434/http://susangardner.com/page3.html">&quot;gluing things&quot;</a> to the front of her Cobble Hill home as a therapeutic exercise after 9/11. She has since transformed her home at 108 Wyckoff Street into the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170203043434/http://untappedcities.com/2012/12/12/the-mosaic-house-of-cobble-hill-brooklyn/">much admired</a> mosaic mecca of the neighborhood, a &quot;celebratory statement that would cheer and amuse&quot; passersby and locals alike. It is a community arts project with a take-a-penny leave-a-penny ethos&#x2014;only instead of pennies, there are colorful trinkets, shiny beads, discarded DVDs, and anti-Trump signage.</p>

<p>It&apos;s impossible to miss Gardner&apos;s glittering home. Plenty of people put up <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170203043434/http://gothamist.com/2014/10/21/brooklyns_bloodiest_halloween_displ.php#photo-1">impressive</a> and <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170203043434/http://gothamist.com/2013/10/21/photos_boerum_hill_residents_want_t.php#photo-1">edgy</a> displays for Halloween and Christmas&#x2014;but Gardner&apos;s home is a year-round celebration. &quot;The neighborhood has embraced and encouraged the project,&quot; she wrote on her website. &quot;The joy of sharing it with strangers has made me very happy. The materials include shells, beads, buttons, jewelry, tiles, mirrors, broken cups/dishes and much more.&quot;</p>

<p>Now with the 15 year anniversary of her project (and September 11th) upon us, we visited the Mosaic House to get a closer look at the display. Check out those photos above, and a short video profile of Gardner below.</p>
