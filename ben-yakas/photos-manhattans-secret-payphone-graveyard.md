</div></div>					<p><br>
We all know that <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150428085141/http://gothamist.com/tags/payphone">payphones</a> are going the way of the beeper; some are being replaced with <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150428085141/http://gothamist.com/2012/11/20/sad_old_pay_phones_beg_for_attentio.php">touchscreen ones</a>, some are being <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150428085141/http://gothamist.com/2013/03/25/payphones.php">repurposed as art projects</a>, but the vast majority <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150428085141/http://gothamist.com/2012/12/05/nyc_wants_your_help_to_reinvent_the.php">are just disappearing</a> little by little. If you&apos;re wondering what happens to some of these phones, look no further than upper Manhattan. </p>

<p><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150428085141/http://www.flickr.com/photos/ennuipoet/9052416771/in/set-72157634144976475">Photographer Dave Bledsoe</a> found a veritable payphone graveyard underneath the elevated West Side Highway at 135th and 12th Avenue: &quot;At least one hundred old, battered pay phones were locked behind a fence near the Park&apos;s Department building.&quot; Check out some photos of them above, and appreciate the fact he used his good camera for this one: &quot;I almost took these on my iPhone, but my irony levels are dangerously low these days.&quot;</p>
