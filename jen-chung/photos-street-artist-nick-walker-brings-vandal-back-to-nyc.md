					<p>You&apos;ve probably seen those wall murals of a dapper besuited man in a hat with the word &quot;Vandal,&quot; but you might not know that <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160605064752/http://www.theartofnickwalker.com/">Nick Walker</a> is the artist behind them. Known within the street art community, Walker is branching out with a huge new mural and a solo gallery show opening today. He told us, &quot;If you want to be an artist, and you want to do and live off your art, then you have to take a stab at it, and paint in the best city in the world, which is New York as far as I&apos;m concerned.&quot;</p>

<p>Walker got his start with graffiti back in Bristol, England in the 1980s and creates murals, stencils and prints. The new show, <em>All I Ever Wanted Was My Name On Fire</em>, is, he said, &quot;the continuation of the Vandal character storyline... I&apos;m also entering into a more abstract version of this particular kind of storyline, kind of fragmenting it and also continuing with the main ethos of what he does. There&apos;s the prankster in him that is kind of being shown more and more within.&quot; And a press release asks, &quot;If you look at each <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160605064752/http://www.theartofnickwalker.com/">Morning After painting</a> as a cover for a novel then this show is the pages within the book. Does he live or die?&quot;</p>

<p>Walker frequently comes to New York, painting his own work or collaborating with other artists. (He was even commissioned by Stanley Kubrick<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160605064752/http://www.independent.co.uk/arts-entertainment/art/features/move-over-banksy-meet-the-next-generation-of-artists-coming-up-from-the-street-776509.html?action=gallery&amp;ino=14"> re-create graffiti&apos;d New York</a> for <em>Eyes Wide Shut</em>.) &quot;There&apos;s so many people here, it&apos;s a massive opportunity for any artist to come here and have their work seen on a regular basis by thousands of people,&quot; he enthused. He also loves seeing other artists&apos; work, &quot;I have the worst attention span ever. I&apos;ll be walking around, I&apos;m like, &apos;Oh, look at that! Look at that wall!&apos;, &apos;Oh, it&apos;s a chance to check out that new piece&apos;&quot; or I absorb it. I&apos;m constantly sort of, my eyes are everywhere...  I&apos;m always on the lookout. There&apos;s loads of brilliant artists in New York.&quot;</p>

<p>Like the TATS CRU. &quot;I&apos;ve always known of their work and stuff, always being very inspired by what they were doing, they&apos;re the part of the proper old school writers of New York. It&apos;s funny how you career goes and things take place, because often, you find yourself... meeting up and painting with the people that actually inspired me and stuff, which is amazing,&quot; Walker said. &quot;I feel kind of blessed to be called up by those guys and say, &apos;Hey look, do you want to <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160605064752/http://tatscruinc.blogspot.com/2014/09/hall-of-fame-2014.html">join me on this painting</a>?&apos;&quot;</p>

<p>Because they both use stencils, his work is <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160605064752/http://gothamist.com/2010/05/21/some_pieces_that_could_be_banksys_b.php#photo-9">sometimes mistaken for Banksy</a>. Walker shrugged it off, saying, &quot;It&apos;s annoying, but it&apos;s just something you kind of learn to live with. I think a lot of people think that there&apos;s only one stencil artist in the world, and because obviously he&apos;s a complete household name these days, and he&apos;s done a lot of amazing projects.&quot; He added that he appreciates street art enthusiasts who try to correct attribution, &quot;I try not to get too pissed by it because we&apos;re both from Bristol, we know each other, and it&apos;s just one of those things.&quot;</p>

<p>While the outside world becomes more fractured, Walker doesn&apos;t think his work has become more political&#x2014;though he does occasionally comment on current events. &quot;I kind of went down that road once, around about 2010 when [then-French President] Sarkozy wanted to ban the hijab in Paris, and the timing on it was just the most important thing for me because I was thinking from, like, you can&apos;t do that,&quot; he said. &quot;That&apos;s kind of as simple as like rights and religious rights to dress the way they want to dress and stuff... There&apos;s no telling what people are capable of doing in politics these days.&quot;</p>

<p><iframe src="https://web.archive.org/web/20160605064752if_/https://www.flickr.com/photos/chrixcel/4450565564/player/" width="640" height="366" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen webkitallowfullscreen="" mozallowfullscreen="" oallowfullscreen="" msallowfullscreen=""></iframe></p>

<p>&quot;I ended up painting I think seven women in burqas doing the cancan, and, it was really a hot, political time,&quot; Walker recalled. &quot;But it was weird... it was embraced by both sides. So you have to see the Islamists thoughts: that they liked it, that they thought it represented liberty, freedom of speech, freedom to do what you want. And on the other side, there&apos;s the far right wing thinking it was something that was in their favor, which they welcomed.&quot; </p>

<p>Still, he prefers to be &quot;a dreamer... I like to think of things a little more far flung and outlandish.&quot;</p>
