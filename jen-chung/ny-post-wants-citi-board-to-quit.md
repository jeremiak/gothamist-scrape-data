                            <p><img alt="2008_11_citifools.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20130901175926im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/jen/2008_11_citifools.jpg" width="130" height="145" class="right">With the feds coming to <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130901175926/http://gothamist.com/2008/11/24/government_in_talks_to_bail_out_cit.php">Citigroup&apos;s rescue with a $20 billion investment</a> and $300+ billion in asset-backing (not to mention the $25 billion it got from the first $700 billion bailout), the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130901175926/http://www.nypost.com/seven/11252008/postopinion/editorials/bounce_these_bozo_bankers_140652.htm">NY Post calls for the heads</a> of Citi&apos;s board.  With a <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130901175926/http://www.nypost.com/seven/11252008/photos/new0b.jpg">graphic detailing board members</a> and their compensation, the Post writes, &quot;Blame abounds, but most of it must accrue to the Citigroup directors - the men and women paid well to make corporate policy, and to oversee its proper execution... <strong><em>At best</em>, they snoozed through the company&apos;s massive build-up of bad debt and rancid security instruments</strong>,&quot; with many shots aimed at former Treasury Secretary Robert Rubin, who has made $107 million from Citi since 1999.  Related: The NY Times&apos;s &quot;<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130901175926/http://www.nytimes.com/2008/11/25/business/25citi.html?ref=business">Saving Cit May Create More Fear</a>&quot; article notes the new bailout might &quot;encourage banks to take more risks in the belief that the government will step in if they run into trouble.&quot;</p>
