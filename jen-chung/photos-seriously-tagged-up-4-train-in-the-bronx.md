</div></div>					<p>It&apos;s pretty rare for tagged-up subway trains to make it out of the rail yards, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150504130925/http://gothamist.com/2013/06/12/tag.php">but sometimes</a> <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150504130925/http://gothamist.com/2013/01/10/photo_graffiti-laden_subway_spotted.php">they do slip through</a>. This morning, a 4 train that emerged at the Woodlawn stop in Bronx was covered in graffiti.</p>

<p>Reader <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150504130925/http://instagram.com/donnquistador">Jose Donneys</a> spotted the trains after 8 a.m., noting, &quot;NYPD and MTA seemed pretty pissed about it!&quot;  He adds that subway was returned to the yards immediately and it took about 10 minutes for a new (non-tagged) train to arrive.</p>

<p> While it&apos;s unclear who is behind the tagging, most graffiti on subways is <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150504130925/http://gothamist.com/2006/12/11/vandalay_graffi.php">made by Europeans</a>. Our in-house expert, Jake Dobkin, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150504130925/http://gothamist.com/2013/01/10/photo_graffiti-laden_subway_spotted.php">explained earlier this year</a>, &quot;Europeans do love to come to NYC and try to get up on a train. Most New York writers don&apos;t, because they know how quickly the trains will get buffed.&quot;<br>
</p>
