<p><img alt="2008_09_govpat2.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20111117115813im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/jen/2008_09_govpat2.jpg" width="130" height="113" class="right">After allowing troubled insurer A.I.G. <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111117115813/http://www.nytimes.com/2008/09/16/business/16aig.html?hp">to borrow $20 billion</a> from itself (and emphasizing that &quot;No taxpayer dollars are involved&quot;), Governor Paterson turned his attention to what the Wall Street woes might mean for the state as a whole.  And he <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111117115813/http://www.ny1.com/content/top_stories/85859/paterson-may-call-lawmakers-back-to-albany--again/Default.aspx">told NY1&apos;s Dominic Carter</a>, &quot;We don&apos;t know what the damage of the events that occurred this weekend is.<strong> I would not be surprised if the budget deficit we just cut down may skyrocket back up and I may have to call on the Legislature to come back and grapple with it again.</strong>&#x201D;  (Last month, Paterson convened an <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111117115813/http://gothamist.com/2008/08/17/paterson_legislature_to_start_worki.php">emergency session</a> to discuss the state&apos;s budget problems since the deficit grew $1.4 billion in just a few months.) <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111117115813/http://www.ny.gov/governor/press/press_0915081.html">In a statement</a> yesterday, Paterson pointed out that 20% of NY State&apos;s revenue comes from Wall Street.</p>
