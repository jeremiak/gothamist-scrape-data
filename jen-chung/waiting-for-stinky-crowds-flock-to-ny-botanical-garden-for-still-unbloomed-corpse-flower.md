					<p>Crowds flocked to the New York Botanical Garden this weekend in hopes of <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/http://gothamist.com/2016/07/21/smell_it_stinky_corpse_flower_set_t.php">seeing the <em>Amorphophallus titanum</em> flower</a> transform into its magnificent, stinky form after being coddled and nursed for 10 years. However, nature refused to cooperate with everyone&apos;s schedule and the corpse flower refused to open.</p>

<p>The NYBG has a livecam of the plant in the Enid Haupt Conservatory, and has been updating the public as best it can on its social media (<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/https://www.facebook.com/NYBotanicalGarden/?fref=ts">Facebook</a>, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/https://www.instagram.com/nybg/">Instagram</a>, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/https://twitter.com/NYBG/status/757322221115375616">Twitter</a>). A statement on the NYBG website explained, &quot;After a weekend of anticipation, the high temperatures in New York did not impact the Corpse Flower&apos;s growth as our experts anticipated. The plant is still progressing, but its bloom remains difficult to predict.&quot;</p>

<p>Photographer Sai Mokhtari, who was at the conservatory yesterday, tells us that the explanations weren&apos;t enough for a &quot;few really entitled millennials (sorry but so true), who were acting offended that the thing didn&apos;t bloom for them.&quot; They weren&apos;t offended by the smell though, as it won&apos;t permeate the room until it opens. <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/http://news.nationalgeographic.com/2015/09/150930-stinky-corpse-flower-chicago-botanic-garden-blooms/">National Geographic</a> tried to describe the smell that awaits visitors:</p><blockquote>To humans, the corpse flower is one of the world&apos;s stinkiest plants. But to dung beetles and flies, it smells like opportunity. &quot;It makes them think there&apos;s rotten meat somewhere to lay their eggs, and then that helps the corpse flower to get pollinated,&quot; [said] Mo Fayyaz, the greenhouse and garden director at the University of Wisconsin&apos;s department of botany...<p></p>

<p>Its distinctive perfume comes from a number of different molecules that smell bad on their own, and that together draws flies, beetles, and people to the plant&#x2019;s cup-like bloom. One of these, timethylamine, smells like rotting fish. Another, isovaleric acid, is the cheesy, sweaty odor responsible for terrible gym sock smells.</p></blockquote>Ahh, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/http://gothamist.com/2016/07/25/and_the_heat_goes_on.php">perfect for our heat wave</a>&#x2014;flora that finally captures that <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/http://gothamist.com/2016/07/14/nyc_summer_smells.php">essence of garbage wind</a>.<p></p>

<p>One NYBG staffer <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161021125016/http://nypost.com/2016/07/24/botany-fans-are-making-a-big-stink-about-this-corpse-flower/">told the Post</a> that employees were &quot;told to put Vicks in our nose if we think the smell will bother us.&quot; And we hear that workers may also use masks, and rotate their shifts more frequently so no one has to be with the smell for too long.</p>

<p>Watch the livestream&#x2014;the corpse flower will open for up to 36 hours and then shrivel up...</p>
