					<p>As New York City continues to wilt its <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016013251/http://gothamist.com/2016/08/14/its_still_hot.php">way through a stifling heat wave</a>, it&apos;s appropriate to remember that things could be worse: 13 years ago today, a massive 30-hour blackout began, thanks to an overgrown tree branch <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016013251/http://gothamist.com/2003/11/20/blackout_report_blame_ohio.php">in Cleveland</a>. The blackout heavily impacted eight states in the Northeast and Ontario.</p>

<p><img src="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016013251im_/http://gothamist.com/upload/2015/08/2015_08_blackout1.jpg" width="640" height="380" alt="2015_08_blackout1.jpg"><br>
<span class="photo_caption">Grand Central Terminal on August 14, 2003(Getty Images)</span></p>

<p>In the Big Apple, stoplights failed and <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016013251/http://gothamist.com/2003/08/15/the_new_york_city_blackout_edition.php">New Yorkers took matters into their own hands</a> and directed traffic. Many had to trudge down the stairwells of their office skyscrapers, and head home&#x2014;if they had a way of getting home, that is.</p>

<p><img src="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016013251im_/http://gothamist.com/upload/2015/08/2015_08_blackout2.jpg" width="640" height="426" alt="2015_08_blackout2.jpg"><br>
<span class="photo_caption">People without hotel rooms in Midtown (Getty Images)</span></p>

<p>Or they just had a really great day, getting cheap or free food and drinks. Many restaurants decided to have impromptu parties, rather than let the food rot in their refrigerators.</p>

<p><span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> </span></p><div class="image-none"> <img alt="2016_08_blackoutsky1.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016013251im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/jen/2016_08_blackoutsky1.jpg" width="640" height="346"> <br> <i> August 14, 2003 (Getty Images)</i></div> <p></p>

<p>Many people piled into buses, but many trapped underground weren&apos;t so lucky. Think about being trapped in a subway! There were 413 subway train sets stopped with 400,000 passengers that had to be evacuated, a process that took almost three hours. A <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016013251/http://ntl.bts.gov/lib/jpodocs/repts_te/14023_files/14023.pdf">federal report noted</a>, &quot;Unfortunately, the passengers flowing into the streets from underground met a massive amount of congestion in the streets and on the sidewalks due to the volume of vehicles and pedestrians.&quot;</p>
