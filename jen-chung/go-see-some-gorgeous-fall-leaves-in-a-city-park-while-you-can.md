					<p>Our <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016020157/http://gothamist.com/2016/10/17/july_in_october.php">schizophrenic autumn</a> (thanks, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016020157/http://gothamist.com/tags/climatechange">climate change</a>!) is here, and the window to do some leaf peeping will be &quot;brief.&quot;</p>

<p>Earlier this week, the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016020157/http://www.nytimes.com/2016/10/25/nyregion/new-york-today-fall-foliage-leaves-changing-world-series.html?_r=1">NY Times noted</a> we were getting <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016020157/http://www.nybg.org/fall_foliage/index.php">closer and closer to peak foliage</a>, and Deanna Curtis, the curator of woody plants at the New York Botanical Garden, said, &quot;We predicted the fall color would be bright and brief, and that seems to be holding true.&quot; Why? Because of this summer&apos;s drought.</p>

<p>&quot;A lack of rain in the Northeast will cause many trees, especially shallow-rooted ones like maples or birches, to go into survival mode, &apos;shutting down&apos; early and preparing for winter. This stressful condition causes leaves to turn brown and fall off before they reach their peak color. The leaves that do change color will do so very briefly and with more muted colors than normal,&quot; <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016020157/http://www.cnn.com/2016/09/20/us/weather-fall-foliage-forecast/">CNN explained</a>.</p>

<p>Photographer <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171016020157/http://www.navidbaraty.com/">Navid Baraty</a> visited Prospect Park and Central Park to see how the trees were doing earlier this week&#x2014;he reported that the trees in Central Park were pretty green, but seemed poised to look amazing this weekend or next week. And Central Park Tweeted yesterday that the time is now for the sugar maples!</p>
