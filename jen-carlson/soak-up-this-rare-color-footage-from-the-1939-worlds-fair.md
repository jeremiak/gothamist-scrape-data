										<p><span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> <img alt="WFcolor39.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20160809232342im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/arts_jen/WFcolor39.jpg" width="640" height="341" class="image-none"> </span></p>

<p>This 25-minute film features stunning footage in full color from the 1939-40 <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160809232342/http://gothamist.com/tags/World&apos;sFair">World&apos;s Fair</a>. The footage was taken from some of the remaining attendees of the Fair, and one man explains he was 24 years old when he finally went on the final day. &quot;I don&apos;t know what possessed me, but I decided to buy 400-feet of 16mm color, and color had just come out. I walked my feet off taking pictures.&quot; </p>

<p>There&apos;s a definite tone of nostalgia and wonderment in their remembrances&#x2014;&quot;It was like looking into the future,&quot; one man says. And filmmaker Amanda Murray describes it as being &quot;a modernist, techno-utopia landed [that] in New York, rocketing kids from the Depression into &apos;The World of Tomorrow.&apos;&quot;</p>

<p>The magical land&#x2014;typically seen now <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160809232342/http://gothamist.com/2015/01/13/1939_worlds_fair_photos.php#photo-1">through b&amp;w; photos</a>&#x2014;was sent up in Flushing Meadows-Corona Park, there was an <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160809232342/https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IND_World%27s_Fair_Line">IND World&apos;s Fair Line</a> to bring people in, and those benches you see people sitting on were <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160809232342/http://gothamist.com/2016/02/25/nyc_bench_history_shocker.php">created by Robert Moses</a>.</p>
