					<p>Currently residing at LIC&apos;s <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161110093421/https://www.nycgovparks.org/parks/hunters-point-south-park">Hunter&#x2019;s Point South Park</a> is a new piece of public art best viewed after dusk (or around dawn). Mika Tajima&apos;s &quot;Merdian (Gold)&quot; offers a colorful display of smokey mist set against the Manhattan skyline. The piece itself is kind of like a mood ring, with fluctuating hues of magenta and pale cyan&#x2014;the color shifs in relation to &quot;the global sentiment for gold, reflected in the price fluctuation of the commodity.&quot; Look a little deeper and you&apos;ll also see commentary on how material things are meaningless and will leave you empty and alone. Sad! But true!</p>

<p>From <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161110093421/http://sculpture-center.org/exhibitionsExhibition.htm?id=113237">SculptureCenter</a>, the not-for-profit arts institution that&apos;s been around since 1928 and commissioned the piece.</p><blockquote>Gold is a peculiar material that derives its value from the social perception of its qualities and collective &#x201C;moods&#x201D; toward geopolitical and economic events. The framing structure for Meridian (Gold) is a seating zone that references communal rejuvenation spas and here becomes a site to reflect on the fleeting materiality of contemporary life. The project was selected by participants in Public Process, SculptureCenter&#x2019;s intensive program for high school students that uses New York City&#x2019;s rich public art landscape to explore the history and impact of public art and its community influence.</blockquote><p></p>

<p>As you&apos;ll see in these photos, you can stand inside of it, so you should be seeing a lot of this on your Instagram feed soon.</p>
