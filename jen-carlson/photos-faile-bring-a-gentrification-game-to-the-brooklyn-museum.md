</div></div>					<p>The Brooklyn Museum saw two big openings this week&#x2014;both their <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151019082922/http://gothamist.com/2015/07/09/sneaker_brooklyn_museum.php">tribute to The Sneaker</a> and FAILE&apos;s big colorful show opened yesterday. FAILE is a collaboration made up of Brooklyn-based artists Patrick McNeil and Patrick Miller, whose prayer wheels used to get <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151019082922/http://gothamist.com/2009/04/25/faile_1.php">stolen from the streets of Williamsburg</a>. They now have a lot more $tatus.</p>

<p>With this show, called <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151019082922/https://www.brooklynmuseum.org/exhibitions/faile/">Savage/Sacred Young Minds</a>, &quot;raises questions about our relationship to consumer culture, religious traditions, and the urban environment by blurring the boundaries between fine art, street art, and popular culture.&quot;</p>

<p>The exhibit is housed on the Museum&apos;s 5th floor, and works include multimedia installations, large-scale paintings, and &quot;sculptures that appropriate imagery from a wide variety of sources, including American quilts, folk and Native American art, religious architecture, pulp magazines, comic books, sci-fi movie posters, adult entertainment advertisements, and storefront typography.&quot; </p>

<p><span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> <img alt="phpEmiyRDAM.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20151019082922im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/arts_jen/phpEmiyRDAM.jpg" width="640" height="427" class="image-none"> </span><br>
<span class="photo_caption">Detail of the gentrification game. (Photo by <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151019082922/http://todseelie.com/">Tod Seelie</a>/Gothamist)</span></p>

<p>There&apos;s also The FAILE &amp; B&#xC4;ST Deluxx Fluxx Arcade, an intensely bright interactive environment created in collaboration with Brooklyn artist B&#xE4;st. There you&apos;ll find arcade games, pinball machines, and foosball tables&#x2014;all sculptures, but interactive ones that you can actually play (for free). The games includes a gentrification video game meant to be played with others, everyone racing to knock down the old Brooklyn buildings and replace them with new condos.</p>

<p>Elsewhere, you&apos;ll find a temple that looks to contain the bust of <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151019082922/http://gothamist.com/2014/12/22/bojack_horseman_what.php">BoJack Horseman</a>.</p>
