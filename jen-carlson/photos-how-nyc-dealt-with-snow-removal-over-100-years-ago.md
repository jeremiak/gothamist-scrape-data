					<p>There was <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://gothamist.com/2010/12/29/blizzard_of_1899.php">the great blizzard of 1899</a>, trains chugged over the snowy Brooklyn Bridge <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://gothamist.com/2009/12/21/flashback_snowy_commute_circa_1888.php">a decade before that</a>, and even more snow buried New York City streets and structures around the turn of the 19th century. Back then, there was no warning when a blizzard was on the way, so the city would have to scramble to deal with clean-up.</p>

<p>Snow removal was a daunting task at the time, without the more <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://gothamist.com/2015/02/12/hot_tub_snow_machine.php">sophisticated equipment</a> that we have today, removing snow meant shovels and carriages. They were still testing out methods of removal, and it wasn&apos;t always quick and efficient&#x2014;in 1898, in a <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://query.nytimes.com/mem/archive-free/pdf?res=9A07E0D71E3DE433A2575AC0A9649C94699ED7CF">letter to the editor</a> of the NY Times about &quot;the snow removal problem,&quot; the reader noted: &quot;Whether the tardiness in removing the snow from the city&apos;s streets is due to the lack of executive ability of the Street Cleaning Department or to the inefficiency of the force and the materials at that department&apos;s command, is an open question.&quot;</p>

<p>For the most part, you would see masses of snow shovelers out on the street (in <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://query.nytimes.com/mem/archive-free/pdf?res=9404E5DF173DE733A25750C0A9659C946497D6CF">this NY Times article</a> from 1905, one is noted to have &quot;fainted from exhaustion&quot;), who would load snow into horse-drawn carriages. From there, the snow would be dumped in the East River (which is <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://gothamist.com/2011/02/03/dont_we_not_dump_snow_in_the_river.php">not allowed anymore</a>).</p>

<p>By 1902, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=9C0DEFDE163AE733A25756C1A9649D946397D6CF&amp;legacy=true">the Times</a> reported that the Department of Street Cleaning had 2,500 &quot;of its own men and 1,500 hired helpers, [who] undertook the problem of removing from the city streets of snow and ice which had accumulated.&quot; Too bad they didn&apos;t have <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170212163649/http://gothamist.com/2014/02/14/fight_snow_with_fire.php">flamethrowers</a>, but a decade or so later they did get some motorized plows.</p>
