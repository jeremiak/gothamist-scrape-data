					<p>Many decades ago, New Yorkers would leave their offices during a meal known as &quot;lunch&quot; and give themselves about one full hour to locate, enjoy, and digest said meal. This purposeful portion of the weekday was called a &quot;lunch break.&quot;</p>

<p>There was no Seamless back then, so these humans had to go out in broad daylight and interact with other humans in order to procure their food, before re-entering the office, where there were no screens to stare at and life was more vivid and 3-dimensional. Photographer <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170526150636/http://www.charlestraub.com/">Charles Traub</a> captured these lunch breaks in the 1970s, and his images are striking, perhaps because these days a similar photo series would contain sad photos of desk lunches and crumbs falling in keyboards. Also, the &apos;70s were an amazing time for fashion.</p>

<p>Traub tells us &quot;a majority [of the photos] were taken at the corners of 57th Street and 5th Avenue,&quot; while others were &quot;taken around the Village, around 6th Ave and 10th Street, and a few on upper Madison Avenue.&quot; Throughout his documenting, Traub ran into many people, including Jackie Kennedy Onassis, and &quot;my own mother who I briefly didn&apos;t recognize.&quot; He also made life-long friends, which can likely be credited to the up-close nature of his shots&#x2014;he always got permission and talked to his subjects before photographing them.</p>

<p>Click through for a look at Traub&apos;s images (which he tells us he plans to exhibit here soon, but you can buy <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170526150636/http://www.amazon.com/dp/8862084234/?tag=slatmaga-20">the book</a> now). You won&apos;t see Onassis in there, however, because Traub wasn&apos;t interested in celebrity. When the two crossed paths, she told him, &#x201C;If you want to take my picture, please be quick.&quot; He declined.</p>
