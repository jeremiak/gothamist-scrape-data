                            <p><span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> </span></p><div class="image-right"> <img alt="ROCKETman.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20130908043728im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/arts_jen/ROCKETman.jpg" width="321" height="269"> <br> <i style=" width:321px; ;display:block">LADEE</i></div> There&apos;s nothing more tragic than when we know something cool is happening in the skies above and we can&apos;t see shit because of light pollution, but announcements like this still fill us with hope until the eventual disappointment. <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130908043728/http://www.space.com/22651-nasa-moon-launch-visible-east-coast.html">Space.com</a> reports that East Coasters &quot;have a good chance to catch a glimpse of a rocket that will be sending a NASA space probe to the moon&quot; tomorrow night.<p></p>

<p>The Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer (<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130908043728/http://www.space.com/22452-ladee-nasa-moon-mission.html">LADEE</a>) is the size of a sedan, and is headed on a mission that includes checking on moon dust conditions.</p>

<p>Blast-off is at 11:27 p.m. EST on Friday, September 6th, and it will launch from Wallops Island, Virginia. <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130908043728/http://www.universetoday.com/104468/historic-sept-6-virginia-moon-shot-heralds-revolutionary-new-paradigm-for-fundamental-science-query-nasa-director-interview/">Universe Today</a> promises the nighttime launch &quot;will be visible to millions of spectators across a wide area of the Eastern US&#x2014;weather permitting,&quot; and they even included a rocket elevation map:</p>
