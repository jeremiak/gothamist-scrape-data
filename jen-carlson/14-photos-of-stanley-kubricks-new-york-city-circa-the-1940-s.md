</div></div>					<p>Arthur Fellig, aka Weegee, collaborated with Stanley Kubrick when the two were both struggling photographers in the city. Weegee&apos;s work from his time behind the lens is being <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150429074047/http://gothamist.com/2012/01/04/grisly_crime_scene_photos_from_1930.php#photo-1">celebrated this year</a>, but where&apos;s the love for Kubrick&apos;s photos? Back in the 1940s he was a staff photographer for LOOK Magazine, and yesterday the Museum of the City of New York <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150429074047/https://twitter.com/#!/MuseumofCityNY/status/194831651065430016">Tweeted</a> a link to photos of Kubrick&apos;s photographs capturing New York City&#x2019;s subway commuters. </p>

<p><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150429074047/http://mcnyblog.org/2012/04/24/a-ride-on-the-subway-in-1946-with-stanley-kubrick/">They note</a>, &quot;Kubrick was not the first photographer to depict the New York City subway.  In 1938 Walker Evans shot many amazing portraits of unknowing riders with a camera hidden in his coat. This may have influenced Kubrick&#x2019;s work.&quot;</p>

<p>The museum has over 8,000 photos from Kubrick <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150429074047/http://collections.mcny.org/C.aspx?VP3=SearchResult_VPage&amp;VBID=24UP1GYEWDNA&amp;SMLS=1&amp;RW=1840&amp;RH=863">in their archives</a>&#x2014;click through for a preview, including those from his subway shoots, and that time a monkey pushed a dog around in a baby carriage at a UN party.</p>
