					<p>On Monday night the summer solstice collided with a full moon, bringing out a rare sight: the Strawberry Moon. The last time this happened was nearly 50 years ago, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108030237/https://www.yahoo.com/tech/first-time-since-1967-start-145553341.html">in 1967</a>, kicking off the Summer of Love. It&apos;s not slated to happen again until 2062. </p>

<p>The Strawberry Moon is called such because, according <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108030237/http://www.accuweather.com/en/weather-blogs/astronomy/strawberry-moon-to-fall-on-summer-solstice-for-first-time-since-1967/58243441">to Accuweather</a>, &quot;the Algonquin tribes knew that it signified that fruits, such as strawberries, were ripe for the picking.&quot; But the color of the moon, while not strawberry-hued, is a little unique&#x2014;its closer proximity to earth &quot;forces its light through thicker air, which also tends to be humid this time of year, and the combination typically makes it amber colored,&quot; according to <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108030237/http://www.almanac.com/content/strawberry-moon-june-full-moon-phases-2016">The Old Farmer&apos;s Almanac</a>. </p>

<p>Where strawberries weren&apos;t native, it had been referred to as the Long Night Moon (romantic!), and the Rose Moon or Honey Moon (for its color&#x2014;did you see <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108030237/https://www.instagram.com/p/BG5ZoAwQa71/?taken-by=michelledozois">that golden sunset</a> it mingled with on Monday?). In the future it will likely be called the Rose Gold Moon, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108030237/http://www.wired.com/2016/04/everybody-mom-obsessed-rose-gold/">because trends</a>.</p>

<p>The event <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108030237/http://live.slooh.com/stadium/live/june-solstice-full-moon">was livestreamed</a>, and you probably saw some awful photos of a blurry dot in your Instagram feed, but now the professionals have shared their gorgeous, clear captures&#x2014;so if you missed it with your own eyes, click through for a glimpse (or wait 46 years).</p>
