</div></div>					<p><span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> <img alt="OLDDINER1013.jpeg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20150422144323im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/arts_jen/OLDDINER1013.jpeg" width="350" height="314" class="image-right"> </span>That beloved, abandoned diner on the West Side Highway may have a &quot;horrific stench,&quot; but it draws in plenty of passersby despite that. Recently, Hannah Frishberg got inside the structure, which (given the rotting food she spotted) is likely being inhabited by squatters. Here&apos;s a look at the inside of the forgotten lunchbox, and below, a little bit about the place, located at 357 West Street.</p>

<ul><li>The diner first opened in the 1950s, operating as different diners throughout time, until finally shuttering in 2006. During <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150422144323/http://www.agilitynut.com/diners/nyc.html">its lifetime</a> it was the Terminal Diner, the Lunchbox Diner, Rib, Seafood Organic, and the Lost Diner. 
</li><li>According to <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150422144323/http://www.scoutingny.com/?p=5772">Scouting NY</a>, it was &quot;designed in the 1950&#x2032;s by the Kullman Building Corporation, famous for their iconic diner designs throughout the northeast.&quot;
</li><li>Preservationist Michael Perlman (he who saved the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150422144323/http://gothamist.com/2008/04/06/last_look_at_th.php">Cheyenne</a> and <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150422144323/http://gothamist.com/tags/MoondanceDiner">Moondance</a> diners) attempted to save this one in 2008, but that never went beyond some paperwork.</li></ul>
