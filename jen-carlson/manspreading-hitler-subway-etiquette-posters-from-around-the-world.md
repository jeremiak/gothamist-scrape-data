					<p>The Transit Museum has just opened their latest exhibit, &quot;Transit Etiquette or: How I Learned To Stop Spitting and Step Aside in 25 Languages,&quot; and it features some fantastic manspreading (and other <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171022171324/http://gothamist.com/tags/subwayetiquette">subway etiquette</a>) campaigns dating back to 1940s NYC, 1970s Japan, and 1980s London. </p>

<p>&quot;Given the nature of urban mass transit&#x2014;small spaces shared with perfect strangers and everyone in a rush&#x2014;it is no surprise that transit agencies have from their very beginnings encouraged passengers to be safe, clean, and well-behaved,&quot; they announced ahead of the exhibit. And yet here we are, still fighting against <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171022171324/http://gothamist.com/tags/manspreading">manspreaders</a>, pole huggers, and those truly awful humans who clip their nails inside of a subway car. </p>

<p>Click through for a look at how other cities have tried to stop the &apos;spread, and then some, over the years&#x2014;Japan even used Hitler character!&#x2014;and check out the exhibit, which runs through July 10th at the New York Transit Museum&#x2019;s Gallery Annex and Store at Grand Central (<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171022171324/http://web.mta.info/mta/museum/#general">more info here</a>).</p>
