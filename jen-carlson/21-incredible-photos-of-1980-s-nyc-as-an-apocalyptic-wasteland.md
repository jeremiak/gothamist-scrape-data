					<p>When some people picture NYC in <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170603180640/http://gothamist.com/tags/1980s">the 1980s</a>, they picture a city in flames, needles and trash swirling around like tumbleweeds, fights on every street corner, and <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170603180640/http://menrule.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/18-escape-from-new-york.jpg">Kurt Russell</a> with an eye patch, spraying a machine gun at the sky. This is one way of looking at the Bad Old Days, even if it&apos;s not 100% the way things were. </p>

<p><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170603180640/http://gothamist.com/2012/01/18/amazing_photos_of_1980s_new_york_ci.php#photo-1">Steven Siegel did a fantastic job</a> of capturing the grit stuck to this city&apos;s past, however&#x2014;through this series of photos shot in the 1980s, he shows the five boroughs as a desolate wasteland. Cracked windows, abandoned scars, empty streets, broken down train cars scatter the landscape. It&apos;s pure post-apocalyptic porn, but it&apos;s all real.</p>

<p><span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> <img alt="16new5seigelpics0112.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20170603180640im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/arts_jen/16new5seigelpics0112.jpg" width="640" height="450" class="image-none"> </span><br>
<span class="photo_caption">Photo by <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170603180640/https://www.flickr.com/photos/stevensiegel">Steven Siegel</a></span></p>

<p>Siegel shot all over the city in the 1980s, and <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170603180640/http://gothamist.com/2012/01/30/steven_siegel_tells_us_about_his_19.php">spoke to us</a> about the difference between then and now. Gather around, children:</p><blockquote>New York in the 1980s differed in two fundamental ways from the New York of today. First, 1980s-era New York was an edgier, riskier, dirtier, tenser, more dangerous and chaotic place. I think that fairly comes through in my images. Second, 1980s-era New York had a sense of wide-openness and freedom that was lost following 9/11... and likely never will be regained. <p></p>

<p>Notice how these two fundamental changes overlap in a number of important ways. A safer city, to some extent, comes at the price of a loss of freedom and openess. Conversely, the edginess and riskiness of the 1980s came at an appalling human and social cost. My photos of South Bronx and Bushwick are&#x2014;if I might say so&#x2014;a testament to that. Those who might be nostalgic for the edginess and riskiness of the 1980s were surely not the people who were growing up in the South Bronx and Bushwick in the era. </p>
