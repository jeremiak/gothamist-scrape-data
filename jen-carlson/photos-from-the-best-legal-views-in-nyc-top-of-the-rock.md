					<p><em>The views around New York City&#x2014;whether you&apos;re on the ground or 100-floors over it, whether you&apos;re a tourist or native&#x2014;are spectacular to behold, and you don&apos;t need to be an urban explorer leaning precariously over a ledge to enjoy some of the best ones. Over the next week, we&apos;ll be visiting some of the highest legally-accessed views around Manhattan&#x2014;<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940/http://gothamist.com/2016/07/11/legal_highs_1wtc.php#photo-1">we previously went over to 1WTC</a>, and today we&apos;re visiting Top of the Rock. Click through for a look at the views from 70 floors up, and below are some facts about the building.</em></p>

<p><strong>7 Facts About Top of the Rock</strong><br>
</p><ul><li>The observation decks (there are three levels) are on top of the tallest structure in Rockefeller Center, 30 Rock.<br>
</li><li>It was first opened to the public in 1933.<br>
</li><li>The decks will bring you 850 feet above the street, and were modeled after the decks on an ocean liner featuring Adirondack-style chairs.</li></ul><br>
<span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> <img alt="newrockcenterflashback0211.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/arts_jen/newrockcenterflashback0211.jpg" width="640" height="449" class="image-none"> </span><br>
<span class="photo_caption"><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940/http://gothamist.com/2011/02/17/flashback_rockefeller_center.php#photo-1">See more old photos here</a>. (Courtesy of Rockefeller Plaza)</span><br>
<ul><li><a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940/https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunch_atop_a_Skyscraper">That famous photo</a> of construction workers lunching on a beam suspended in the air was taken during the building of 30 Rock, originally called the RCA Building.<br>
</li><li>The building was designed by architect Raymond Hood, who <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940/http://www.theatlasphere.com/columns/090907-heynick-rockefeller.php">some believe</a> was the inspiration for Peter Keating in Ayn Rand&#x2019;s <em>The Fountainhead</em>.<br>
</li><li>The popular deck closed in 1986, and only reopened in 2005 <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940/https://www.rockefellercenter.com/blog/2015/10/28/top-rock-turns-10/">as Top of the Rock</a>.<br>
</li><li>You can also get some great views five floors down, at the bar <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940/http://gothamist.com/2015/04/15/sixtyfive_terrace_rainbow_room.php">in the Rainbow Room</a>.</li></ul><p></p>

<p>Tickets to the Top of the Rock <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161108025940/https://www.topoftherocknyc.com/ticket-menu/">start at $32</a>.</p>
