</div></div><p>We&apos;ve seen 1970s New York City cops <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20120504164634/http://gothamist.com/2011/10/15/7_photos_of_cops_and_criminals_laug.php">laughing it up on the job</a>, but the Bad Old Days weren&apos;t <em>always</em> so hilarious. Starting this week the Museum of the City of New York is unveiling their latest exhibit, titled <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20120504164634/http://www.mcny.org/exhibitions/current/Police-Work-Leonard-Freed.html">Police Work: Photographs by Leonard Freed, 1972-1979</a>. The expansive collection of vintage prints from the Brooklyn-born photographer show &quot;life on the beat&quot; during the &quot;tumultuous&quot; time, revealing &quot;the complexity, the harshness, and the camaraderie of the city&apos;s public safety servants and the people they protected.&quot; </p>

<p>The photos first appeared in the Sunday Times in 1973, with the headline: &quot;Thugs, Mugs, Drugs; City In Terror.&quot; Click through for a little look at the exhibit.</p>
