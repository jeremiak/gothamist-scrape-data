					<p>Every year New Yorkers stand in the middle of busy Manhattan streets to capture a shot of the sun perfectly aligning with our street grid. This phenomena is known as <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170716132436/http://gothamist.com/tags/manhattanhenge">Manhattanhenge</a>, and was discovered and named by Neil deGrasse Tyson, who also holds the copyright. The event happens just four times a year, and the American Museum of Natural History just announced the 2017 dates.</p>

<p>MONDAY, <strong>MAY 29</strong>, AT 8:13 PM (HALF SUN)<br>
TUESDAY, <strong>MAY 30</strong>, AT 8:12 PM (FULL SUN)</p>

<p>WEDNESDAY, <strong>JULY 12</strong>, AT 8:20 PM (FULL SUN)<br>
THURSDAY, <strong>JULY 13</strong>, AT 8:21 PM (HALF SUN)</p>

<p>The museum notes that prime viewing locations include &quot;large cross streets such as 14th, 34th, 42nd, 57th, and 79th.&quot; They also recommend arriving 30 minutes ahead of time to stake out a spot and set up.</p>

<p>On May 30th, there will be a public program (starting at 7 p.m.) &quot;highlighting the history and astronomy of this spectacle.&quot; It will take place <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170716132436/http://www.amnh.org/learn-teach/adults/hayden-planetarium-programs/manhattanhenge">at the Hayden Planetarium</a> and feature Jackie Faherty, a research associate in the Museum&#x2019;s Department of Astrophysics.</p>
