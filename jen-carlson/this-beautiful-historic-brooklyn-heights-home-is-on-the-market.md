					<p>Brooklyn Heights, &quot;America&apos;s First Suburb,&quot; is home to some of the more enviable domiciles in the city. Among the carriage houses and former mansions-turned-co-ops, there are a number of wooden structures, and one of them is now on the market <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170124154301/http://www.bhsusa.com/brooklyn/unique-federal-era-house-and-garage/townhouse/15940392#floorplan">for $10.5 million</a>.</p>

<p>Situated behind Truman Capote&apos;s old apartment (<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170124154301/http://gothamist.com/2012/03/21/truman_capotes_brooklyn_heights_hom.php">inside 70 Willow Street</a>, which he would sometimes insinuate he owned, but didn&apos;t), stands a 19th Century Federal-style house at 13 Pineapple Street, which the author wrote about in &#x201C;A House on the Heights.&#x201D;</p><blockquote>I&#x2019;m not much acquainted with the proper history of the Heights. However, I believe (but please don&#x2019;t trust me), that the oldest house, the house still extant and functioning, belongs to our back-yard neighbors, Mr. and Mrs. Philip Broughton. A silvery gray, single-wood Colonial shielded by trees robustly leafed, it was built in 1790, the home of a sea captain.</blockquote>According to the listing, the home is filled with period charm, wood burning fireplaces, bay windows, and other things you really don&apos;t need to hear about unless you have $10.5 million. It&apos;s also right near <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170124154301/http://gothamist.com/2012/07/16/the_brooklyn_promenade.php#photo-1">the Promenade</a>, which you can likely get a partial view of from one of those bay windows. [via <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170124154301/http://ny.curbed.com/2017/1/19/14319672/brooklyn-heights-truman-capote-townhouse-for-sale">Curbed</a>]<p></p>
