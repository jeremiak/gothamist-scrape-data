					<p>The bistro format will never go out of style&#x2014;who could get sick of steaming bowls of mussels and steak frites?&#x2014;and there&apos;s another in the city&apos;s fleet of classic French restaurants at  <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161218161627/http://boucherie.nyc/">Boucherie</a>, open now in the West Village. Everything&apos;s in its right place: the tiled floors and walls, the expansive mirror behind the bar, the slightly wobbly wooden chairs circled around cafe tables. The menu doesn&apos;t reveal any surprises, but why should it? That&apos;s not why you&apos;re at a bistro to begin with.</p>

<p>Executive Chef Jerome Dihul, who can be seen running between the restaurant&apos;s two open cooking spaces, was Chef de Cuisine at Keith McNally&#x2019;s Pastis prior to its <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161218161627/http://gothamist.com/2014/02/28/pastis_closes_tonight_for_15-month.php">closing</a>. If there&apos;s one restaurateur synonymous with French cuisine in NYC, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161218161627/http://gothamist.com/2016/11/04/augustine_keith_mcnally_beekman_hot.php#photo-1">it&apos;s McNally</a>.</p>

<p>Escargots ($19) drowned in butter, as they should be, make for a delectable if calorically-heavy starter (ask for some more baguette to soak up all the garlic, lemon and parsley sauce). At a recent press preview, the Steak Frites au Poivre ($36) was more than enough to share, with a generous heap of extra crispy fries and a sliced 14-ounce New York strip with a creamy peppercorn sauce. </p>

<p><em>99 7th Avenue South, 212-873-1616; <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20161218161627/http://boucherie.nyc/">boucherie.nyc</a></em></p>
