					<p>Prohibition-era glamorization has everyone dubbing their establishment a speakeasy, whether it&apos;s simply <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171021004818/http://gothamist.com/2015/09/04/hell_phone_bushwick.php#photo-1">through a discreet door</a> or <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171021004818/http://gothamist.com/2013/05/20/chelsea_water_tower_transformed_int.php#photo-1">perched on a rooftop</a>. Speakeasy is really just catch-all term that roughly translates to &quot;pricy drinks&quot; at this point. We haven&apos;t heard the term associated with restaurants as often, but <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171021004818/http://www.dinnertable.nyc/">dinnertable</a>, a new East Village eatery opening on Wednesday, has declared itself a &quot;reverse speakeasy,&quot; whereby a street-level bar conceals the entrance to a 20-seat restaurant hidden in the back. </p>

<p>The new restaurant will be accessible through Garret East on Avenue A, an extension of a sister bar in the West Village that <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171021004818/http://gothamist.com/2014/03/26/the_garret.php#photo-1">opened above a Five Guys</a> about two years ago. The menu&apos;s rife with gourmet comfort food, like the Smoked Shortrib Tartare ($12) with horseradish and marble rye, &quot;The Dumplings&quot; ($15), a riff on pierogi made with potato and pecorino cheese, and a nod to their fast food space sharers across town with The Sixth Guy&apos;s Burger ($16) made Italian with capocollo, straccino cheese and deli peppers. And that incredible-looking baked pasta? That&apos;s the Lasagna Bolo, a two-person dish for $38.</p>

<p>Drinks skew Italian in the back (Peronis, bottled negronis and Italian wines, apparently) while it&apos;s more about modern cocktail culture up front. The restaurant only operates Tuesday through Sunday from 5:30 p.m. until 11 p.m. and they won&apos;t accept either reservations or parties exceeding four people. </p>
