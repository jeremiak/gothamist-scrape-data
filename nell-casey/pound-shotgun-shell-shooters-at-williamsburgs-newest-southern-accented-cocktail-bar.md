					<p>The shots-and-beers of Williamsburg&apos;s <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160821060839/http://gothamist.com/2016/01/13/rip_williamsburgs_good_co.php">chill dive bar</a> Good &amp; Co. have been replaced with some highfalutin cocktails of a Southern persuasion with the opening of Belle Shoals inside the Hope Street space. Beverage director Pamela Wiznitzer from sister restaurant Seamstress and head bartender James &quot;Jimbo&quot; Palumbo&#x2014;who was shaking things at <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160821060839/http://gothamist.com/2015/12/31/best_drink_2015.php">The Up &amp; Up</a> before defecting to Brooklyn&#x2014;are doing some intriguing things with alcohol, including serving shooters out of shotgun shells and toasting marshmallows as garnishes. Even Serious Cocktail People like to have fun once in a while.</p>

<p>Watching the preparation for the Auntie Bellum ($13) should inspire an order if for no other reasons than fun-sized marshmallows are adorable and it&apos;s a kick to be served your own airplane bottle of booze as a garnish. Inside the ice, amontillado sherry, coconut syrup, tiki bitters and lemon, to which you&apos;ll add Grand Marnier to taste. There&apos;s no fire involved in the Raisin Hell ($12), despite the name, but the combination of Cutty Sark Prohibition whiskey with Jager, Italian vermouth and sherry will put a warming feeling in your belly.</p>

Executive chef Aaron Lamonica&apos;s keeping the food options tight, channeling some NOLA flair with an Oyster Po&apos;Boy ($15) and more oysters inside the Hell&apos;s Belle&apos;s ($14), serrano-wrapped fried bivalves tucked into an herb crepe with smoked aioli and pickled onions. The chef employs the same smokey mayo to accompany a mess of crunchy shoe string fries ($7), one of several fried options that also include excellent Jalapeno Hush Puppies ($7), served with a bourbon butter and spicy honey at a recent press preview.
<br><br>
Instead of chicken&#x2014;Williamsburg has quite enough of that, thank you&#x2014;Lamonica employs duck leg and skate wing (fried, of course) as proteins for his buttery biscuit sandwiches. The former ($16) makes for a messy but satisfying meal. Duck has a more intense, gamey flavor than its clucking counterpart; it&apos;s also exceedingly rich when deep fried, which is where the accompanying sour pickles and crock of coleslaw come to the rescue.
<br><br>
The space has been spiffed up a bit and there are some new tables and a snazzy-looking jukebox as the focal point, but otherwise the bones of Good &amp; Co live on. The <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160821060839/http://gothamist.com/2016/01/13/rip_williamsburgs_good_co.php">enormous side yard</a> hasn&apos;t debuted yet, but when it does there&apos;ll be some different food options including mint juleps and more oysters.
