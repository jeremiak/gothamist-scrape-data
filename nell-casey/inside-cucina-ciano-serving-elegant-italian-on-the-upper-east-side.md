                            <p>After just two-and-a-half years in the Flatiron, Stratis Morfogen <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20140208071548/http://ny.eater.com/archives/2013/04/ciano_abprubtly_shutters_gallante_rumored_to_have_left.php#more">shuttered</a> his <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20140208071548/http://gothamist.com/2010/11/11/new_restaurants_16.php#photo-5">restaurant Ciano</a>, promising he would reopen again soon on the Upper East Side. The time has come and Morfogen opens the revamped <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20140208071548/http://www.cucinaciano.com/">Cucina Ciano</a> this evening with former Ciano sous chef Tin Huynh behind the line. Like its predecessor, Cucina Ciano offers unpretentious yet refined Italian fare, but Morfogen aims to attract a regular neighborhood crowd as opposed to the &apos;special occasion&apos; clientele at the former restaurant.</p>

<p>Chef&apos;s Huynh&apos;s dishes speak to that mentality. Though polished on the plate, the food radiates simple comfort, as though prepared in a rustic Tuscan trattoria. At a recent press preview, the exquisitely moist Veal Meatballs ($14) came blanketed in a perfectly balanced arrabiata sauce, with just enough bite to cut through the tang of the tomato. A simple Panzanella Salad ($14) covered all the texture categories from creamy, melty burrata to crunchy, well-toasted bread chunks. Tender strozzapreti pasta are tossed in a velvety Duck Bolognese ($15/25), a dish made popular at the restaurant&apos;s first incarnation. The enormous Pork Chop ($26) could feed two&#x2014;or one seriously hungry patron. Order it medium or less (yes, you can do this with quality pork); the juicy pork mixes with the earthy bean ragout and escarole, creating a delicious plate gravy for dipping.</p>

<p>The cheery dining room comes aglow with candlelight for the evening meal, creating a romantic yet relaxed environment. While the dim lighting might set a certain mood, the music skewed towards contemporary indie rock on our visit, though played in hushed tones so not to overwhelm the atmosphere. It&apos;s a little like eating in a well-to-do friend&apos;s UES townhouse; it&apos;s not stuffy, but there&apos;s an undeniable sophistication, courtesy of Morfogen&apos;s designer wife Filipa Fino, who worked as Accessories Editor at Vogue before turning to interior design. </p>

<p>We didn&apos;t get to see it, but a secret dining space is found in the subterranean level; with room for 18, it doubles as a wine room and a private party room complete with a sound system and television.</p>

<p><em>181 East 78th Street, (646) 422-7550; <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20140208071548/http://www.cucinaciano.com/">cucinaciano.com</a>. Open for dinner Monday through Sunday 5 p.m. to 12 a.m.</em></p>
