					<p>Last Friday night, Staten Island-born chef Albert Di Meglio opened <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170624045927/http://www.baranobk.com/">Barano</a> on Williamsburg&apos;s Southside, inside a decade-old condo on Broadway near Kent Avenue. It&apos;s the chef&apos;s first solo venture after stints in the kitchens of Le Cirque, Osteria Del Circo and, most recently, Rubirosa, sibling of SI&apos;s famed Joe &amp; Pat&apos;s Pizzeria. Lured by the promise of fresh-pulled mozzarella and wood-fired pizzas, I gathered a group of Staten Island friends to join me for an opening night meal at Di Meglio&apos;s new restaurant. <br>
 <br>
&quot;This isn&apos;t the kind of stuff people eat on Staten Island,&quot; one of them laughed at the meal&apos;s completion. </p>

<p>That&apos;s not to say there aren&apos;t spots to get freshly made Italian foods in Shaolin, but I knew what he meant. In a borough that favors its Italian-American heritage, a dish of Octopus al Piastra ($17), enthusiastically flavored with pickled radish and mint pesto, would be something of an anomaly. Same with a Spit-Roasted Lamb Leg ($26), with Northern African influences like golden raisins and roasted carrots and the Italian staple pine nuts. Those dishes were the highlights of our meal and a delicious flex of Di Meglio&apos;s culinary muscles.</p>

<br><br>
For this venture, Di Meglio&apos;s focusing on southern Italian flavors and ingredients, like Neapolitan-esque pizzas, whose crusts a server told us were a mixture of durham and semolina flours with a wild yeast. They&apos;re highly flavorful, even when competing with salty pancetta on the Bosciaola ($18), which also comes topped with mushrooms, mozzarella and pecorino. In a unique&#x2014;and, honestly, somewhat impractical&#x2014;twist, diners are given fancy shears to cut their own slices, a difficult task on the metal grates each pie is perched atop.
<br><br>
Di Meglio&#x2014;who still lives on Staten Island with his family&#x2014;also embraces his red sauce roots, offering Meatballs ($14) and an Eggplant Parmigiana ($12), where the nightshade vegetable is roasted (instead of fried) and topped with tomato, mozzarella, basil and parm. &quot;Growing up on Staten Island, life was about food. In my family, you&apos;re happy you eat, you&apos;re sad you eat, you&apos;re angry you eat,&quot; Di Meglio explains. &quot;My grandmother was always cooking something. We didn&apos;t do anything without food. Sundays was always a banquet in my house, 25 people coming over eating artichokes, eating meatballs.&quot;
<br><br>
Another menu item, Bucatini with Rabbit alla Ischitana ($17), also speaks to the chef&apos;s childhood. &quot;Every year I would get a pet rabbit, and every year right before Easter it would run away. Every Easter we would eat this awesome &apos;chicken&apos; dish,&quot; he recalls. &quot;Little did I know... this annual &apos;tradition&apos; inspired the Rabbit all Ischitana we have on the menu at Barano.&quot;
