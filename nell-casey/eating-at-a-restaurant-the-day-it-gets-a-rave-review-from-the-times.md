					<p>&quot;We&apos;re all feeling pretty cocky,&quot; a beaming Le Coucou staffer told me last night, a fitting sentiment for a restaurant whose mascot is a strutting chicken. Earlier that day, the restaurant learned it had been <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171021004420/http://www.nytimes.com/2016/11/02/dining/le-coucou-review.html?_r=0">awarded three stars</a> by the Times&apos;s Pete Wells, a <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171021004420/https://twitter.com/shitfoodblogger/status/793585358546108416">notable distinction</a> for a new restaurant (only two other NYC eateries in their infancy have been awarded thusly this year). Luckily, I&apos;d managed to obtain a reservation some three weeks prior, unwittingly choosing the night the restaurant was apt to be at its most festive. &quot;We had champagne before service,&quot; the staffer confided.</p>

<p>Le Coucou opened this summer under the direction of chef Daniel Rose, a Chicago-born chef who had the fortitude and daring to open a French restaurant, Spring, in Paris. Now he&apos;s on the border of Chinatown on Lafayette Street, doing &quot;old-guard French,&quot; as Wells puts it, partnering with prolific restaurateur Stephen Starr on a restaurant that toes the line between stately and modern. Be guarded with your gesticulating, lest you upend the tall candle on your table.</p>

<p>Some might translate Rose&apos;s vision as stuffy French&#x2014;the tall white chef&apos;s hats donned by the kitchen staff both confirm and disprove this notion&#x2014;and in many ways that&apos;s not an incorrect assumption. The French menu is rife with preparations like terrines and remoulades, sweetbreads and foie gras. The prices alone are indication enough that well-to-do septuagenarians are like to be the restaurant&apos;s bread and butter, so to speak, though last night&apos;s crowd was a thorough mix of David Yurman, puffy Polo vests, and <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20171021004420/http://www.racked.com/2016/3/11/11195958/tv-hair-jane-the-virgin-empire-younger">artfully tousled ringlets</a>. </p>

<br><br>
Rose&apos;s food feels nostalgic even if you weren&apos;t weaned on duck and rabbit; there&apos;s a yearning for a time when French food was the most lofty culinary goal, sitting atop the food chain of gourmet cuisines. As Wells notes, the kitchen uses a prodigious amount of butter, apparent in the foamy and luxurious sauce am&#xE9;ricaine accompanying the Quenelle de Brochet ($29), a Lyonnaise dish of creamed pike, whipped with eggs and poached &apos;til it resembles a firm cloud. 
<br><br>
The Cr&#xE9;pinette de Volaille ($25) were another showstopper of the meal, the rustic chicken and foie gras sausage arriving with a cap of roasted pluot and an extra shaving of raw foie. As previously noted, diners with an aversion to offal will be at a disadvantage, as multiple dishes on the menu are fortified with foie, while others employ tongue, pancreas and even a veal&apos;s head as the star. Even the complimentary bread comes with both butter and spiced pork fat. Unsurprisingly, a meal here can lead to heavy eyes, depending on what you order.
<br><br>
But last night, there was no room for sleeping amidst the restaurant&apos;s justified glee, and judging by how difficult it was to score a reservation <em>before</em> the <em>Times</em> weighed in, there&apos;s no doubt the party will continue for the foreseeable future. But it will be rare to recapture last night&apos;s level of jubilation, especially after the marching band interrupted service for a Bruno Mars rendition.
