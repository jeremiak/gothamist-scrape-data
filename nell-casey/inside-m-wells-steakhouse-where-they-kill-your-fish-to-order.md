</div></div>					<p>It&apos;s been an exciting wait for Hugue Dufour and Sarah Obraitis&apos;s newest whimsical restaurant offering in Queens, following the closure of their first <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509204149/http://gothamist.com/2012/03/22/m_wells_is_dead_long_live_m_wells_d.php">ridiculously popular destination restaurant M. Wells</a> in 2012. The mini <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509204149/http://gothamist.com/2012/03/22/m_wells_is_dead_long_live_m_wells_d.php">PS1 Dinette offshoot</a> sated the city&apos;s desire for creative Canadian fare in the meantime, but the new full-service <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509204149/http://magasinwells.com/">M. Wells Steakhouse</a> offers the whole shebang inside a converted garage (natch) in Long Island City. They may not <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150509204149/http://gothamist.com/2012/10/05/never_mind_m_wells_says_neigh_to_ho.php">serve horse meat</a> but the dishes are still anything but ordinary. </p>

<p>It&apos;s the meat that matters here, from the Bone-in Chateaubriand for two ($100) to the Stack of Pork Chops ($28) topped with umami-rich anchovy butter. There&apos;s always some kind of burger on a steakhouse menu, and much fuss has been made over the Bone-in Burger ($17) here, a coarsely patty served on a bun with a giant bone attached for gnawing. It&apos;s the most Instagrammable dish on offer, but it&apos;s the freshly prepared Truine au Bleu (trout) that truly impresses. The wriggling fish are plucked from an on-site tank to order, gasping their final breath just minutes before diners pluck the tender flesh from the bone. </p>

<p>A steakhouse can be equally judged on its sides and they&apos;re not resting on the rote creamed spinach and mash here. Potatoes can be ordered in a cheesy, fondue-esque crock (Pommes Aligote, $12), thin-sliced and baked (Pommes Boulangerie, $10) or in Canada-friendly Poutine ($10), which sees crisp fries drowned in savory brown gravy and squeaky cheese curds. Escargot ($12) gets a meaty punch with the addition of bone marrow while Gnocchi ($15) get a decadent infusion of foie gras. If fish is more your style, opt for the BBQ Mussels on a Stick ($8), a smokey, briny version of yakitori, or the gelatinous Scungelee ($12) stuffed with different kinds of seafood. </p>

<p>The space retained some of its car-storage charm, like the metal roll down gates and unfinished floor. Bright red walls add drama to the dining room while wallpapered ceilings and vintage-looking chandeliers add touches of old world elegance. The most fun seats in the house are in front of the open kitchen, where doomed trout swim in a large sink and fiery logs create heat for searing big hunks of flesh. There&apos;s drama at the bar, too, with a blow torch creating a flambeed marshmallow topping for a seasonal egg nog cocktail. </p>
