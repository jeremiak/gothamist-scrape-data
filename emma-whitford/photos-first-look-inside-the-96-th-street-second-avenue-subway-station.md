					<p>Reporters, construction workers and a few lucky members of the public crammed the northern entrance of the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/tags/secondavenuesubway">Second Avenue Subway&apos;s</a> 96th Street station on Thursday, applauding as Governor Andrew Cuomo and MTA Chairman Thomas Prendergast descended the escalator from street level. </p>

<p>For many, the whirlwind afternoon press tour marked a first glimpse of the bright, vaulted, rat-free station. &quot;There&apos;s high ceilings, there&apos;s column-free designs, and there&apos;s an airy feeling&#x2014;unlike what you see in the majority of the subway system,&quot; Prendergast said. </p>

<p>The northernmost Second Avenue station to date, 96th Street marks the end of Phase I of the project, along with two other new stations at 72nd Street and 86th Street. The line has been in the works for <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2016/12/21/2nd_avenue_subway_history.php">almost a century</a>, and cost $4.5 billion to date. </p>

<p>&quot;The train is going to run if we have to push the train down the track,&quot; Cuomo told the crowd, Thursday. &quot;It was important that we meet the deadline. Why? Because we wanted to make a statement.&quot; </p>

<p>It&apos;s been almost a decade since the project&apos;s most recent groundbreaking, and <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2016/11/15/anythings_possible_sadly.php">delays</a> have been frequent. The governor himself has been checking in regularly for about <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/13/nyregion/second-ave-subway-andrew-cuomo.html">the last year-and-a-half</a>. </p>

<p>Congresswoman Carolyn Maloney heaped praise on the station. &quot;This is an achievement that is right up there with the construction of the pyramids as a marvel of what human kind can build,&quot; she said, standing before a large <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2016/12/19/second_avenue_subway_has_exciting_p.php#photo-1">porcelain tile mural</a> by artist Sarah Sze that recalls paper blowing in the wind. &quot;Only the pyramids don&apos;t have this artwork!&quot; </p>

<p>She was also the only politician to explicitly mention the project&apos;s <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2015/11/04/de_blasio_2nd_ave_subay.php">next phase</a>, which will extend the long-awaited line up to 125th Street in East Harlem. &quot;Governor Cuomo has already put into the budget $1.5 billion for the next phase of the Second Avenue Subway,&quot; she said. &quot;We will not be waiting 100 years for the next phase.&quot; </p>

<p>That $1.5 billion has <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2016/04/21/money_in_money_out.php">fluctuated in recent years</a>. Announcing his finalized 2016-17 budget in March, Governor Cuomo promised to restore the full $1 billion cut from phase two last fall, albeit in stages&#x2014;$500 million in 2019, and a minimum of $443 million in the next Capital Plan, covering 2020-24.</p>

<p>William Henderson, director of the watchdog group <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://www.pcac.org/">Permanent Citizens Advisory Committee to the MTA</a>, told us this spring that such commitments can be dubious. &quot;It&apos;s in the state&apos;s financial plan, but only next year&apos;s budget is binding, so what does &apos;commitment&apos; mean here?&quot; he said. &quot;It&apos;s basically [like saying] &apos;my word is good.&apos;&quot;</p>

<p>With the first $500 million, the MTA hopes to get preliminary engineering work for phase two underway. Tunnel boring still isn&apos;t expected to begin until after 2019.</p>

<p><span class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-image" style="display: inline;"> </span></p><div class="image-none"> <img alt="2016_12_press2nd.jpg" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705im_/http://gothamist.com/attachments/jen/2016_12_press2nd.jpg" width="640" height="427"> <br> <i> A scrum for a subway line decades in the making (<a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://www.gretchenrobinette.com/">Gretchen Robinette</a> / Gothamist)</i></div> <p></p>

<p>Cuomo made no explicit mention of phase two before Thursday&apos;s press conference shifted into its own second phase&#x2014;a borderline dangerous crush of news cameras maneuvering for shots while the Governor, Congresswoman Maloney, and Prendergast stepped ceremoniously into the station (no turnstiles yet, just shiny silver arches). </p>

<p>Taylor Swift&apos;s &quot;Welcome To New York&quot; played over the sound system as the group walked quickly along the platform, passing a kiosk full of subway themed cookies and pausing occasionally for Prendergrast to point out features like the glass, mid-platform elevators and contiguous welded tracks (which are quieter, since the train doesn&apos;t have to clack over welded joints every 39 feet). Cuomo, grinning and waving, took no questions. At one point, he said, &quot;I have no words.&quot; </p>

<p>&quot;I mean, you know this is a big deal that we&apos;re going to see the opening of the Second Avenue Subway,&quot; said <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://www.straphangers.org/">Straphangers Campaign</a> Coordinator Jaqi Cohen on Thursday. &quot;It&apos;s something we&apos;ve been waiting for as New Yorkers for almost 100 years. I&apos;m happy the governor is so enthusiastic. While he&apos;s down moving the Second Avenue Subway along, let&apos;s keep in mind that it&apos;s not the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2016/05/03/sorry_guys_at_capacity.php">only aspect of the subway</a>.&quot; </p>

<p>Mayor Bill de Blasio, who recently <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/https://www.dnainfo.com/new-york/20161213/gramercy/second-ave-subway-de-blasio-cuomo-feud">expressed interest</a> in attending the official January 1st opening of the Second Avenue Subway, was not present at Thursday&apos;s congratulatory press tour. While Governor Cuomo controls the MTA, appointing the chairman of its board, NYC has <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2015/10/10/at_last_cuomo_and_de_blasio_have_ag.php">committed billions</a> to the system. Fresh off his latest highly-publicized spat with the governor&#x2014;this time, over the fate of a <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2016/12/16/cuomo_cares_no_really.php">one-antlered deer</a>&#x2014;the mayor sent First Deputy Mayor Anthony Shorris to 96th Street in his stead. </p>

<p>A spokeswoman for Cuomo&apos;s office told Gothamist, without prompting, that de Blasio had been invited on Thursday morning, before the press conference was formally announced (the event was apparently solidified Wednesday night). A de Blasio spokesman said the mayor was otherwise engaged&#x2014;his public schedule for the afternoon included bill signings and a photo op with 16 formerly homeless veterans who moved into affordable housing in the Bronx.</p>

<p><em>The 96th Street station will be open to the public for tours today from 8:00-10:00 a.m., and 5:00-7:00 p.m. The Second Avenue Subway is scheduled to open on January 1st at noon, but regular service won&apos;t start until <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20170313044705/http://gothamist.com/2016/12/20/second_avenue_subway_limited.php">the following week</a>.</em> </p>
